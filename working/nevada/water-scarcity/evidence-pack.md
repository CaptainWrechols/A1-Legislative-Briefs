### 1. Who gets water and how permits are decided (13 bills)

Rules for who may take and use Nevada's water: permit applications, deadlines, temporary changes to existing rights, and exemptions.

- **AB30 (2019)** — Failed, stage: died_in_second_chamber; best floor vote 31-9 (77.5% yes). Would have let the State Engineer require monitoring-and-mitigation plans, with public hearings, before deciding water permit applications.
- **AB62 (2019)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 28-13 (68.3% yes). Tightened deadlines and limited repeated extensions for actually putting permitted water to use.
- **AB354 (2021)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have created state-chartered water banks to hold and manage deposited water (the Nevada Water Banking Act).
- **AB6 (2021)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 42-0 (100.0% yes). Streamlined hearing requirements for applications to temporarily change existing water rights.
- **AB325 (2023)** — Failed, stage: after_origin_committee. Would have let surface-water users make temporary changes within irrigation district boundaries without a full permit application.
- **AB34 (2023)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 21-0 (100.0% yes). Moved State Engineer application notices onto the agency website and relaxed newspaper publication rules.
- **AB90 (2023)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have let the State Engineer approve temporary water-right changes for up to 10 years instead of 1.
- **SB258 (2023)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 42-0 (100.0% yes). Allowed temporary water-right changes of up to 3 years for renewable energy generation projects.
- **AB109 (2025)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have changed how water brought to the surface by geothermal projects is treated under water-appropriation law.
- **AB132 (2025)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 42-0 (100.0% yes). Doubled the allowed size of wildlife water 'guzzlers' that are exempt from water-appropriation permitting.
- **AB190 (2025)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have created a fund so applicants do not bear the full cost of hydrological studies the State Engineer requires.
- **AB265 (2025)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have required notice and timelines when the State Engineer assigns a water application to an internal review committee.
- **AB9 (2025)** — Failed, stage: after_origin_committee. Extended temporary conversions of farm water rights for wildlife or stream flow from 3-year to 5-year increments.

### 2. Rules for underground water and wells (15 bills)

Groundwater-specific rules: over-pumped basins, groundwater boards, well drilling and replacement, and paying for well supervision.

- **AB233 (2019)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 41-0 (100.0% yes). Let counties use general funds, not only special property assessments, to pay well-supervision costs.
- **AB51 (2019)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have required rules for managing groundwater and surface water together (conjunctive management), including mitigation plans.
- **AB95 (2019)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 28-12 (70.0% yes). Set rules for ordering cutbacks in groundwater pumping in basins where withdrawals exceed natural replenishment.
- **SB140 (2019)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 39-0 (100.0% yes). Required the State Engineer to reserve 10 percent of uncommitted groundwater in certain basins so it cannot be appropriated.
- **SB236 (2019)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 39-0 (100.0% yes). Let a property owner sink a replacement well without a full new change application in defined situations.
- **SB149 (2021)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have changed how local groundwater boards operate in designated basins.
- **AB91 (2023)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 20-0 (100.0% yes). Revised the exception that lets owners bore replacement wells without a new change application.
- **SB112 (2023)** — Failed, stage: after_origin_committee. Would have changed the county special assessments that fund well supervisors.
- **SB113 (2023)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 42-0 (100.0% yes). Revised petition rules for groundwater management plans in basins designated as critical management areas.
- **SB176 (2023)** — Failed, stage: after_origin_committee. Would have created an account to buy and retire water rights in over-appropriated groundwater basins.
- **SB180 (2023)** — Failed, stage: after_both_chambers; best floor vote 41-0 (100.0% yes). Would have changed how groundwater boards are established and dissolved.
- **AB363 (2025)** — Failed, stage: after_origin_committee. Would have changed groundwater board membership and operation.
- **AB485 (2025)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have changed county assessments that fund well supervision.
- **SB342 (2025)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have revised how the State Engineer designates groundwater basins for special administration.
- **SB36 (2025)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 42-0 (100.0% yes). Created the Account for Retiring Water Rights to buy and permanently retire rights in over-appropriated groundwater basins.

### 3. Saving water (conservation rules and incentives) (8 bills)

Getting people and businesses to use less water: conservation plans, water-loss audits, efficient fixtures, and limits on decorative grass.

- **AB163 (2019)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 28-12 (70.0% yes). Required water utilities to report water-loss audits with their conservation plans and required WaterSense-certified fixtures in most new construction.
- **AB356 (2021)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 21-0 (100.0% yes). Banned use of Colorado River water delivered by the Southern Nevada Water Authority to irrigate nonfunctional (decorative) grass, among other conservation steps.
- **AJR2 (2021)** — In Progress, stage: passed_both_chambers_enrolled_resolution; best floor vote 21-0 (100.0% yes). Resolution recognizing that healthy forests, rangelands and soils are tied to the amount and quality of water.
- **AJR3 (2021)** — In Progress, stage: passed_both_chambers_enrolled_resolution; best floor vote 26-16 (61.9% yes). Resolution urging various actions to protect and conserve land and water.
- **AB186 (2023)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have required food establishments to serve drinking water only when a customer asks for it.
- **AB191 (2023)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 42-0 (100.0% yes). Broadened which water suppliers must adopt conservation plans, water-loss audits and conservation incentives.
- **AB134 (2025)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have let water-right holders submit voluntary water conservation plans to the State Engineer for approval.
- **SB143 (2025)** — Failed, stage: died_in_second_chamber; best floor vote 21-0 (100.0% yes) [weak title match]. Would have had a legislative interim committee review water conservation and ordered a state study of artificial turf.

### 4. Planning for growth and future water supply (8 bills)

Preparing for growth and drought: local water resource plans, tying development approvals to water, and long-range studies and advisory bodies.

- **AB265 (2019)** — Failed, stage: after_origin_committee. Would have required the Desert Research Institute to study water treatment and recycling and report to the next Legislature.
- **SB150 (2019)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 39-0 (100.0% yes) [weak title match]. Required counties and cities to develop and maintain long-term water resource plans alongside their master plans.
- **SB250 (2019)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 39-0 (100.0% yes). Adjusted when state and local governments can require dedication of water rights before approving new development.
- **SB499 (2019)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have created an Advisory Board on Water Resources Planning and Drought Resiliency inside the state water agency.
- **AB333 (2021)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 21-0 (100.0% yes) [weak title match]. Set deadlines and procedures for lawsuits challenging local land-use planning decisions.
- **SB98 (2021)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 41-0 (100.0% yes). Expanded the Carson Water Subconservancy District into parts of Storey County and adjusted its board.
- **AB261 (2023)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 42-0 (100.0% yes). Required the State Plan for Economic Development to address efficient use of Nevada's water resources.
- **AB385 (2025)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee [weak title match]. Would have stopped Clark County-area governments from using building codes to prohibit or limit water-using cooling towers.

### 5. Colorado River and deals with other states (4 bills)

Nevada's stake in shared rivers and lakes: the Colorado River Commission, interstate compacts, and asks of the federal government.

- **SB433 (2019)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 40-0 (100.0% yes). Updated the California-Nevada compact giving both states law-enforcement jurisdiction on Lake Tahoe and Topaz Lake.
- **SB76 (2019)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have reworked the Colorado River Commission's role in providing Colorado River water and power services.
- **AB15 (2021)** — Failed, stage: origin_committee. Would have changed the membership of the Colorado River Commission of Nevada.
- **SJR3 (2023)** — In Progress, stage: passed_both_chambers_enrolled_resolution; best floor vote 42-0 (100.0% yes). Resolution urging the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation to consider actions and alternatives to protect and manage the Colorado River.

### 7. Clean drinking water and pollution (8 bills)

Keeping water safe to drink and waterways clean: pollution permits, drinking-water systems, septic-to-sewer conversion, and disclosure of well quality.

- **SB276 (2025)** — Enacted, stage: enacted; best floor vote 42-0 (100.0% yes). Revised public notice requirements for water pollution discharge permits.

## Data limits

- Keyword-discovered set: the 88 bills come from water-related keyword searches, not an exhaustive sweep. Bills that affect water without water words in their titles or digests may be missing.
- 18 of 88 bills fail the strong water-title filter; they touch water only partially (boats, boilers, highway naming, food regulation, utility notices). Headline counts should prefer the 70-bill strong-title subset.
- OpenStates detail lookups came back empty for all 88 bills, so every vote, sponsor and action record here comes from a single source: the Legislature's NELIS system.
- Floor votes: 104 final-passage roll calls have full counts and named ballots. Committee records are thinner: 176 of 287 committee meeting entries (hearings and work sessions) have no recorded roll call, so most committee-stage outcomes rest on recommendations and subsequent actions, not counted votes.
- Party labels on floor ballots were matched from the NELIS legislator directory with a Ballotpedia fallback (100% of 3,267 ballot rows matched); a small mismatch risk from name matching remains.
- Introduced-versus-enrolled text comparisons exist only for the 48 Governor-bound bills; failed bills have no amendment-impact analysis here.
- For several failed bills the processed milestone flags mark second-chamber passage even though no second-chamber final-passage roll call exists and the action history shows the bill died in the second chamber (e.g. AB387 of 2023, SB324 of 2025). Stages in this pack therefore count second-chamber passage only when a winning final-passage roll call from that chamber exists.
- The pack contains no fiscal notes, agency impact studies, hearing testimony, or lobbying records; it cannot say why a bill stalled, only where.
- The three 'In Progress' items are joint resolutions enrolled and delivered to the Secretary of State; they express positions or propose constitutional changes and do not become statutes.
- Independent verification (data-verifier, 2026-07-16): overall PASS_WITH_WARNINGS; all bill identifiers, counts and dispositions checked; 20 of 20 sampled official URLs alive; the only warning concerned manifest file layout, not data content.
