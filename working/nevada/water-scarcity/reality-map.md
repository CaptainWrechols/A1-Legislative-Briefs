# Reality Map: Growth, Water Scarcity, and Long-Term Supply in Nevada

Issue: `nevada-04-water-scarcity` | Built by Reality Mapper v2.1 on 2026-07-17 | Machine version: `reality-map.json`

## What this is

A political-reality map built from the evidence pack's 88 water-related bills across Nevada's 2019, 2021, 2023 and 2025 sessions. It describes what was tried, what moved, what stalled, and who showed up. It does **not** say what citizens should choose; it sorts the record into history baskets so deliberating groups can weigh it themselves.

**History baskets used throughout:**

| Basket | Citizen-facing label | Meaning in this dataset |
|---|---|---|
| `often_moved` | "Often moved before" | Similar ideas often became law or cleared both chambers |
| `unfinished` | "Got support but didn't finish" | Majority floor votes or both-chamber passage without enactment; timing/process deaths count |
| `rarely_moved` | "Rarely moved before" | Repeated early stops, especially in origin committee |
| `mixed` | — | Some versions moved, others repeatedly stalled |

## 2. Theme scorecards

Overall, 26 of 40 failures (65%) stopped in the bill's origin committee. Median winning floor margins are near 100% in most themes — when a water bill got to a final-passage vote in this record, it usually passed overwhelmingly.

### Theme 1: Who gets water and how permits are decided — basket: `mixed` (certainty: high)

13 bills (all strong title matches), 5 enacted (38%). Typical stop: origin committee (5 of 8 failures). Median winning floor vote: 100% yes (6 bills with final votes).

- Enacted examples: AB6 (2021) streamlined hearings for temporary water-right changes, 42-0; SB258 (2023) 3-year temporary changes for renewable energy, 42-0; AB34 (2023) moved permit notices online, 21-0.
- Stalled examples: AB354 (2021) water banking, origin committee; AB190 (2025) hydrological-study cost fund, origin committee; AB30 (2019) monitoring-and-mitigation plans before permit decisions, passed the Assembly 31-9 then died in the Senate.

Facts: the enacted bills were narrow or procedural — deadlines, notices, hearing rules, small exemptions — and passed near-unanimously. Bills that added new structures or changed who decides stopped at the first committee.

Inference: in this record, narrow procedural permit changes often moved, while structural changes to permit decision-making rarely cleared the first committee.

### Theme 2: Rules for underground water and wells — basket: `mixed` (certainty: high)

15 bills (all strong), 7 enacted (47%). Typical stop: origin committee (4 of 8 failures), plus the set's one after-both-chambers death (SB180, 2023). Median winning floor vote: 100% yes (8 bills).

- Enacted examples: AB95 (2019) pumping cutback rules for over-drawn basins, 28-12; SB140 (2019) 10% groundwater reserve, 39-0; SB36 (2025) account to buy and retire water rights, 42-0.
- Stalled examples: AB51 (2019) conjunctive management, origin committee; SB180 (2023) groundwater-board restructuring, passed 41-0 and 21-0 and still died; AB363 (2025) groundwater-board membership, after origin committee.

Facts: this is the largest theme and delivered substantive basin-management tools, not just housekeeping. Water-right retirement failed in 2023 (SB176) and passed unanimously two years later (SB36, 2025). Groundwater-board restructuring failed three times across three sessions (SB149 2021, SB180 2023, AB363 2025) — once after clearing both chambers unanimously.

Inference: this theme moved more than most, and at least one idea went from stalled to enacted across sessions; board restructuring is the recurring non-finisher.

### Theme 3: Saving water (conservation rules and incentives) — basket: `mixed` (certainty: medium)

8 bills (7 strong), 3 enacted (38%), plus two enrolled 2021 resolutions. Typical stop: origin committee (2 of 3 failures). Median winning floor vote: 100% yes (6 bills).

- Enacted examples: AB356 (2021) decorative-grass ban for Colorado River water in southern Nevada, 21-0; AB191 (2023) broadened who must adopt conservation plans and water-loss audits, 42-0; AB163 (2019) water-loss audits and WaterSense fixtures, 28-12.
- Stalled examples: SB143 (2025) interim conservation review plus artificial-turf study — passed the Senate 21-0, died in the Assembly; AB186 (2023) water-on-request in restaurants, origin committee.

Facts: the enactments bind utilities and suppliers. The one consumer-facing rule in the set stopped at the first committee.

Inference: supplier-facing conservation mandates repeatedly became law in this record; a single consumer-facing example is insufficient to conclude anything firm about that category.

### Theme 4: Planning for growth and future water supply — basket: `often_moved` (certainty: medium)

8 bills (5 strong), 5 enacted (63%; 3 of 5 within the strong subset). Typical stop: origin committee (2 of 3 failures). Median winning floor vote: 100% yes (5 bills).

- Enacted examples: SB150 (2019) county/city long-term water resource plans, 39-0; SB250 (2019) water-right dedication before development approval, 39-0; AB261 (2023) state economic development plan must address efficient water use, 42-0.
- Stalled examples: SB499 (2019) new drought-resiliency advisory board, origin committee; AB265 (2019) mandated recycling study, after origin committee.

Facts: every enactment passed its best floor vote unanimously. The failures proposed new bodies or studies rather than attaching duties to existing local processes.

Inference: planning requirements tied to existing local processes often moved in this record; new advisory bodies and study mandates stopped early. Sample is moderate; certainty medium.

### Theme 5: Colorado River and deals with other states — basket: `mixed` (certainty: low)

4 bills (all strong), 1 enacted (25%), 1 enrolled resolution. Both failures died in origin committee.

- Enacted: SB433 (2019) California-Nevada law-enforcement compact update for Lake Tahoe and Topaz Lake, 40-0.
- Enrolled: SJR3 (2023) resolution urging the Bureau of Reclamation to protect the Colorado River, unanimous in both chambers.
- Stalled: SB76 (2019) and AB15 (2021), both restructuring the Colorado River Commission, both dead at the first committee.

Inference: the sample is too small for strong conclusions. The only visible pattern — both Commission-restructuring attempts stopping at origin committee — rests on two bills.

### Theme 6: Money for water projects and agencies — basket: `often_moved` (certainty: high)

13 bills (11 strong), 11 enacted (85%) — the highest rate of any theme. Both failures died in origin committee. Median winning floor vote: 100% yes (11 bills; every enacted bill won its best vote at 95%+).

- Enacted examples: AB19 (2023) tribal eligibility for river channel grants, 41-0; SB473 (2023) extreme-rainfall study funding, 42-0; SB472 (2023) South Fork Dam projects, 42-0.
- Stalled examples: SB102 (2023) grant money for local water resource plans; SB159 (2025) money to a UNLV water/wastewater lab — both never left origin committee.

Facts: no funding bill in this set lost a floor vote.

Inference: in this record the filter for water money was the money committee, not the floor.

### Theme 7: Clean drinking water and pollution — basket: `mixed` (certainty: medium)

8 bills (7 strong), 3 enacted (38%). Stops split: 3 origin committee, 2 second-chamber deaths after majority floor votes. Median winning floor vote: 80% yes (5 bills) — the lowest of any theme with multiple votes.

- Enacted examples: AB220 (2023) 100% septic-to-sewer conversion funding, 17-3; AB146 (2021) non-point-source pollution plans, 14-6; SB276 (2025) pollution-permit notice rules, 42-0.
- Stalled examples: AB263 (2023) Legionnaires' disease in building water systems, passed the Assembly 28-14 then died in the Senate; SB324 (2025) Tahoe-area plastic water bottle ban, passed the Senate 16-4 then died in the Assembly.

Facts: this theme holds more of the set's contested floor votes than any other.

Inference: clean-water bills split between early committee stops and second-chamber deaths, and drew real floor opposition more often than other themes.

### Theme 8: Who referees water disputes (courts and the State Engineer's office) — basket: `rarely_moved` (certainty: medium)

6 bills (5 strong), 0 enacted — the only theme with no enactments in any of the four sessions. Stops: 3 origin committee (all 2021), 1 after origin committee, 2 second-chamber deaths.

- Stalled examples: SJR1 (2021) constitutional amendment routing State Engineer challenges to the Court of Appeals, origin committee; AB387 (2023) mandatory best-available-science standard, passed the Assembly 26-14 then died in the Senate; SB31 (2025) vested-claim adjudication deadlines, passed the Senate 15-6 then died in the Assembly; AB419 (2025) public reports on long-pending applications, after origin committee.

Facts: the stopping point moved later over time — origin-committee deaths in 2021, second-chamber deaths in 2023 and 2025 with majority floor votes along the way.

Inference: in this record, changing who reviews the State Engineer never finished. Six bills is enough to note the pattern but not to call it fixed, and the two most recent bills also fit the `unfinished` description (majority votes, no enactment).

### Theme 9: Other bills that touch water — basket: `often_moved` (certainty: medium, relevance: low)

13 bills but only 3 strong title matches; 10 enacted (77%). All 3 failures died in origin committee. This is the keyword-search catch-all — boats, boilers, a highway name, food regulation. Its high enactment rate says essentially nothing about water policy.

Inference: no water-specific conclusion is warranted from this theme.

## 3. People and process signals (descriptive only)

**Who carried bills.** Committees introduced about half the set (45 of 88, mostly Natural Resources and money committees). Among individuals, Pete Goicoechea (R) appeared as a primary sponsor most often — 13 bills across 2019-2023, concentrated in groundwater/wells and planning; his bills include enactments (SB150, SB250 in 2019; SB113 in 2023) and committee stalls (SB102, SB176 in 2023). Howard Watts (D) appeared on conservation and clean-water bills in all four sessions, including enacted AB163 (2019) and SB276 (2025). Rich DeLong (R) appeared on 2023-2025 bills including enacted AB191 (2023) and several 2025 State Engineer process bills that stalled (AB265, AB312, AB419). Bert Gurr (R) appeared on four permit-process bills, all of which stopped in or just after origin committee. James Settelmeyer (R) appeared on four 2019-2021 bills, all enacted. No judgment about effectiveness or intent is implied by any of this.

**Cross-party sponsorship.** Rare: only 4 of 43 person-sponsored bills list both major parties among person sponsors — AB233 (2019, enacted), AB265 (2019, failed), SB236 (2019, enacted), SB276 (2025, enacted). Inference: 3 of 4 were enacted, but four bills is too few to generalize.

**Committee choke points.** 26 of 40 failures (65%) stopped in origin committee, split almost evenly between chambers (14 Assembly, 12 Senate). The pack records the *stage* where bills stopped, not the named committee that held them, and most committee meetings in the underlying data lack recorded roll calls — so this map does not name specific committees as choke points. That attribution is not supported by the data.

## 6. Deliberation prompts (questions, not answers)

1. If an idea often stalled in the first committee — water banking (AB354, 2021), Colorado River Commission changes (SB76 2019, AB15 2021) — what would have to change for it to be realistic next time?
2. SB180 (2023) passed both chambers 41-0 and 21-0 and still never became law. Is that a "no" on restructuring groundwater boards — or a process problem worth distinguishing from opposition?
3. Retiring water rights failed in 2023 (SB176) and passed unanimously in 2025 (SB36). What changed between sessions, and does that history apply to other stalled ideas?
4. Funding bills that reached a floor vote passed at 95-100% yes, but two funding asks (SB102 2023, SB159 2025) never left committee. When the filter is the money committee rather than the floor, what does "support" mean?
5. Every bill about who reviews the State Engineer failed (6 bills, 2021-2025), but the two most recent got majority floor votes before dying in the second chamber. Is that trajectory growing traction, or noise from a small sample?
6. Only 4 of 43 person-sponsored bills had sponsors from both major parties. Does an idea need cross-party sponsors to fit the pattern of what passed here, given that most enactments were committee-sponsored or near-unanimous anyway?
7. 2025 introduced the most water bills (26) and failed the most (16, ten in origin committee). Does a crowded session change how a group would time or bundle an idea?
8. Clean-water bills had the most contested floor votes (median winning margin 80% versus roughly 100% elsewhere). When an idea passes with real opposition, what does that mean for how durable the policy is?
9. This dataset cannot say *why* any bill stalled — no fiscal notes, testimony, or lobbying records. Which of these patterns would a group want to check with people who were in the room before relying on them?

## Data limits carried forward

- Keyword-discovered set; not a proven complete universe of water bills.
- 18 of 88 bills are weak title matches; theme 9 is mostly background noise.
- Single source (NELIS); OpenStates lookups returned empty.
- Stages say where bills stopped, never why; no fiscal notes, testimony, or lobbying records.
- Most committee meetings lack recorded roll calls, so choke points are described by stage, not by named committee.
- The three "In Progress" items are enrolled joint resolutions, which do not become statutes.
- Amendment-impact comparisons exist only for the 48 Governor-bound bills.
