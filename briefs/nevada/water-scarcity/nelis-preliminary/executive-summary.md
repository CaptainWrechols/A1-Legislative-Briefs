### Purpose of this brief

This preliminary brief shows what a completed Legislative Brief Version 0 package looks like using **Nevada Legislature Information System (NELIS) bill-discovery data only**. It synthesizes the **stated purpose and bill language** visible in NELIS titles for water-related measures introduced in the last four regular sessions. It is **not** the final Version 0 brief. A separate, clean Version 0 will be built when OpenStates action, vote, sponsor, and enactment data are ready.

Where NELIS does not provide a fact needed for briefing (committee votes, floor votes, crossover, individual yeas and nays, sponsors, or signed-into-law status), this document states **INSUFFICIENT DATA**.

---

### Context

Nevada’s water policy environment sits at the intersection of Colorado River supply reliability, groundwater basin management, conservation rules, and growth-related demand.[^1] Agency background materials describe Southern Nevada’s reliance on the Colorado River system and related planning pressures.[^2] State planning materials from the Division of Water Resources frame water planning as an ongoing administrative and statutory subject.[^3]

Over the last four regular sessions, lawmakers repeatedly filed measures whose NELIS titles address water governance, groundwater, water rights, conservation, Colorado River institutions, and agency capacity.[^4]

### Synthesis: what the bills appear intended to do

NELIS records in this collection include **identifier, session, title, and source URL**. They do **not** include full bill text, fiscal notes, or floor analyses. The synthesis below is therefore based on **title language** (including Bill Draft Request chapter cues such as BDR 48-… for water statutes). Impact on the ground and enactment effect are **INSUFFICIENT DATA** unless noted as an agency context source.

#### 1. General water statute revisions (core NRS Chapter 48 language)

A large share of titles use stock drafting language—“Revises provisions governing water,” “Revises provisions relating to water,” or “Revises provisions related to water”—signaling omnibus or chapter-level amendments to Nevada water law rather than a single named program.[^4] Examples include AB30, AB51, AB62, and AB95 (2019); AB19 and AB20 (2023); and parallel “revises provisions relating to water” filings in later sessions.[^4]

**Language pattern:** process/statute revision, not a self-contained program name in the title.  
**Purpose (from title only):** amend existing water law.  
**Impact:** **INSUFFICIENT DATA.**

#### 2. Groundwater basins, boards, and management plans

A recurring cluster names groundwater instruments directly: basin use (SB140, 2019), groundwater boards (SB149, 2021; SB180, 2023; AB363, 2025), basin assessments (SB112, 2023), groundwater management plans (SB113, 2023), and groundwater conservation (SB176, 2023).[^4]

**Language pattern:** “groundwater,” “basin,” “boards,” “management plans,” “conservation of groundwater.”  
**Purpose (from title only):** change how basins are governed, assessed, or conserved.  
**Impact:** **INSUFFICIENT DATA.**

#### 3. Water rights, diversion, and dedication

Titles in this group speak to property- and permit-style water interests: change of diversion for certain wells (SB236, 2019), dedication of water rights (SB250, 2019), and temporary change applications for appropriated water (AB6, 2021).[^4] Related titles also address appropriations supporting the Division of Water Resources’ operating capacity (for example SB509, 2019; AB465, 2021; SB102, SB472, SB473, 2023).[^4]

**Language pattern:** “water rights,” “diversion,” “appropriated water,” “dedication,” plus DWR appropriation titles.  
**Purpose (from title only):** adjust rights administration and/or fund the rights-administering agency.  
**Impact:** **INSUFFICIENT DATA.**

#### 4. Water conservation

Explicit conservation titles include AB163 (2019), AB191 and AB220 (2023), and AB134 (2025), which “revise” or “establish” provisions relating to water conservation.[^4] Some other titles mention “conservation” only because they reference the State Department of Conservation and Natural Resources; those are agency-structure or funding measures, not necessarily water-conservation program bills.[^4]

**Language pattern:** “water conservation” vs. incidental “Department of Conservation and Natural Resources.”  
**Purpose (from title only):** for the water-conservation subset, change conservation rules or create conservation provisions.  
**Impact:** **INSUFFICIENT DATA.**

#### 5. Colorado River institutions and federal interface

A smaller set names the Colorado River Commission or federal Reclamation action: SB76 (2019) on CRC services; AB15 (2021) on CRC membership; SJR3 (2023) urging the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation to consider specified Lake Mead / Colorado River protection measures.[^4]

**Language pattern:** “Colorado River Commission,” “Bureau of Reclamation.”  
**Purpose (from title only):** alter CRC structure/services or memorialize a federal request.  
**Impact:** **INSUFFICIENT DATA** (resolutions do not themselves enact NRS chapter amendments).

#### 6. Planning, drought resiliency, and study mandates

Examples include SB499 (2019), creating an Advisory Board on Water Resources Planning and Drought Resiliency, and AB265 (2019), requiring the Desert Research Institute to study water treatment and recycling.[^4]

**Language pattern:** “planning,” “drought resiliency,” “study,” “treatment and recycling.”  
**Purpose (from title only):** create advisory capacity or commission research.  
**Impact:** **INSUFFICIENT DATA.**

#### 7. Legislative Counsel Bureau / agency context (not bill text)

Separate from individual bill titles, collected agency background includes LCB material on data centers, Division of Water Resources planning context, and SNWA supply overview.[^1][^2][^3] Those documents help frame *why* water scarcity appears on the legislative calendar; they do **not** state which of the 147 bills passed.

---

### Patterns visible from titles alone

- **Volume is steady.** Roughly three dozen water-tagged measures appear each session in this NELIS search set, with a higher count in 2025 (42).[^4]
- **Statute repair language dominates.** Generic “revises provisions … water” titles are common, which usually signals incremental NRS change rather than a single named statewide program.[^4]
- **Groundwater and rights language recurs across sessions**, suggesting ongoing basin and rights-administration attention rather than a one-session spike.[^4]
- **Stall points, killed bills, and enacted subset:** **INSUFFICIENT DATA** without action and “became law” records.[^4]

### Assembly viability indicators (preliminary)

Evidence from NELIS titles alone suggests water scarcity and long-term supply remain **active legislative subjects** in Nevada, with recurring drafting around groundwater, rights, conservation, and Colorado River institutions.[^4] Historical pattern of repeated introduction may indicate the topic is **familiar** to lawmakers. Data gaps prevent assessment of enactment rates, bipartisan coalition patterns, or which draft approaches most often survive committee.
