Reading a bill id

Bills appear as a year plus a number, for example 2019 AB163. "AB" means Assembly Bill, "SB" means Senate Bill, and "AJR"/"SJR" mean Assembly or Senate Joint Resolution. Some tables also show a compact machine key such as 80:AB163 — the number before the colon is the legislative session (80th = 2019, 81st = 2021, 82nd = 2023, 83rd = 2025).

A few terms, once

Term Plain meaning
First (origin) committeeThe committee a bill is sent to in the chamber where it starts. Most failures in this set stopped here.
Final passageThe up-or-down floor vote by a full chamber on whether the bill passes.
Second chamberAfter passing its starting chamber, a bill must also pass the other one.
EnrolledThe final, official version of a measure after both chambers pass it.
Joint resolutionA statement or proposed constitutional change; it can pass both chambers but does not become a statute.
ChapterA bill that became law is assigned a chapter number in the session laws.

Where the data comes from

All records come from the Nevada Legislature's public NELIS system, collected and processed locally; party labels on roll calls were matched from the Legislature's legislator directory with a Ballotpedia fallback. The set was assembled through water-keyword searches and is not a proven complete universe of water-relevant legislation. Appendix F lists all known limits.

Appendix F — What This Data Can and Cannot Tell You

Every table in these appendices comes from the same underlying collection of Nevada legislative records. That collection has known limits. Reading this page first helps avoid over-reading the numbers elsewhere.

Limits of the bill set

  1. Keyword-discovered set: the 88 bills come from water-related keyword searches, not an exhaustive sweep. Bills that affect water without water words in their titles or digests may be missing.
  2. 18 of 88 bills fail the strong water-title filter; they touch water only partially (boats, boilers, highway naming, food regulation, utility notices). Headline counts should prefer the 70-bill strong-title subset.
  3. OpenStates detail lookups came back empty for all 88 bills, so every vote, sponsor and action record here comes from a single source: the Legislature's NELIS system.
  4. Floor votes: 104 final-passage roll calls have full counts and named ballots. Committee records are thinner: 176 of 287 committee meeting entries (hearings and work sessions) have no recorded roll call, so most committee-stage outcomes rest on recommendations and subsequent actions, not counted votes.
  5. Party labels on floor ballots were matched from the NELIS legislator directory with a Ballotpedia fallback (100% of 3,267 ballot rows matched); a small mismatch risk from name matching remains.
  6. Introduced-versus-enrolled text comparisons exist only for the 48 Governor-bound bills; failed bills have no amendment-impact analysis here.
  7. For several failed bills the processed milestone flags mark second-chamber passage even though no second-chamber final-passage roll call exists and the action history shows the bill died in the second chamber (e.g. AB387 of 2023, SB324 of 2025). Stages in this pack therefore count second-chamber passage only when a winning final-passage roll call from that chamber exists.
  8. The pack contains no fiscal notes, agency impact studies, hearing testimony, or lobbying records; it cannot say why a bill stalled, only where.
  9. The three 'In Progress' items are joint resolutions enrolled and delivered to the Secretary of State; they express positions or propose constitutional changes and do not become statutes.
  10. Independent verification (data-verifier, 2026-07-16): overall PASS_WITH_WARNINGS; all bill identifiers, counts and dispositions checked; 20 of 20 sampled official URLs alive; the only warning concerned manifest file layout, not data content.

What is missing entirely

  • No fiscal notes, agency impact studies, hearing testimony, or lobbying records are included — the data shows where a bill stopped, never why.
  • Stage labels record the last step a bill reached, not which named committee held it; most committee meetings in the record have no recorded roll call, so committee choke points can only be described at the stage level.
  • Introduced-versus-final text comparisons (Appendix G) exist only for the 48 measures that finished the process; bills that failed have no amendment analysis.
