Growth, Water Scarcity, and Long-Term Supply in Nevada

This sheet shows what Nevada lawmakers tried on water since 2019 — and what happened — so your group can judge what is realistic.

How to use this sheet

  • Read it as history, not as advice.
  • Compare the paths: ideas that often moved, ideas that got stuck, ideas that almost made it.
  • Decide together what matters to you. This sheet does not pick for you.

A session is the months when lawmakers meet to pass laws; Nevada meets every other year.

How a bill moves

A bill is a written idea for a new law. It moves in steps:

start → first committee → floor vote → the other house → the governor

A committee (a small work group of lawmakers) studies the bill first. If the committee says yes, the whole house takes a floor vote — every member votes yes or no. Then the bill repeats those steps in the other house. Nevada has two houses: the Assembly and the Senate. Last, the governor signs it into law. A bill can stop at any step.

One fair caution

This set was found by keyword search. It holds 88 water bills, not every bill in Nevada history. The record shows where each bill stopped — never why. Patterns here describe this record only.

People and process signals

Facts only. No judgment about anyone is implied.

  • Committees themselves put forward about half the bills in this set (45 of 88). A sponsor is the lawmaker or committee that puts a bill forward.
  • Pete Goicoechea (R) was the most frequent person sponsor: 13 bills from 2019 to 2023. Some became law; some stalled in committee.
  • Howard Watts (D) sponsored water-saving and clean-water bills in all four sessions.
  • Bills with sponsors from both major parties were rare: 4 out of 43 person-sponsored bills. Three of those four became law.
  • Failed bills stopped in their first committee most often — 26 of 40 — split about evenly between the two houses.

Passed recently (2025)

The newest session already changed some things. These are facts, not endorsements.

2025 SB36 · Became law

A state account to buy water rights in over-used groundwater areas and retire them for good.

2025 SB276 · Became law

New public notice rules for water pollution permits.

Also new in 2025: AB104 (updated grant rules for resource projects, water included) and AB132 (bigger wildlife water catchers allowed without a permit).

Questions for your group

1

SB180 (2023) passed both houses with zero “no” votes and still did not become law. Was that a “no” to the idea, or a timing problem?

2

Retiring water rights failed in 2023 (SB176) and became law in 2025 (SB36). What changed in between?

3

Most failed bills never got past their first committee. What might help an idea get a hearing and a vote?

4

Every bill about who checks the State Engineer failed — six bills since 2021. Two recent ones (2023, 2025) won floor votes in one house before dying in the other. Is that growing support, or chance?

5

Money bills almost never lost a floor vote, yet two money asks (2023 SB102, 2025 SB159) died in committee. What does “support” mean when the committee is the filter?

6

This record never says why a bill stopped. Who was in the room and could say more?
