A session is the months, every other year, when Nevada lawmakers meet: 2019, 2021, 2023, 2025 in this record.

How a bill moves

start → first committee → floor vote → the other house → the governor

A bill (a written idea for a new law) is studied first by a committee — a small work group of lawmakers. If the committee says yes, the whole house takes a floor vote. Then the steps repeat in the other house, and the governor signs or vetoes. A bill can stop at any step — the record shows where each one stopped, never why.

Interstate pipeline / federal water project No bill. The tool used so far: formal requests. A 2023 resolution urging federal Colorado River action passed 42–0 and 21–0 (SJR3). The requests passed easily. Two tries to restructure Nevada’s own Colorado River agency died in their first committee (2019 SB76, 2021 AB15). The decision sits with Congress, not Carson City. A resolution is cheap to pass; what follows one is the open question.
Regulate data centers
(closed-loop cooling; pay their own way)		 Eleven bills touch data centers or their tax deals. None regulates their water or cooling. Bills helping large users passed 4 of 4 (incl. 2019 SB547). Bills restraining them failed 5 of 5. A tax-break cap passed the Senate 14–7, then died in Assembly Revenue (2023 SB394). A deny-the-tax-break bill died in its first committee (2025 AB77). The one cooling bill pushed the other way — 2025 AB385 would have blocked local bans on big water-cooled towers — and also died in its first committee. No Nevada statute covers data-center water. The southern Nevada cooling ban is a local water-agency rule, not state law. Lawmakers studied data-center water and energy through 2026 for the 2027 session — the next real window.

People and process signals

Facts only; no judgment of anyone is implied. A sponsor is the lawmaker or committee that puts a bill forward.

  • A slight majority of bills (58 of 108) came from committees. Most frequent person sponsor: Pete Goicoechea (R), 14 bills across all four sessions.
  • Sponsor teams from both major parties were rare (5 bills) — and 4 of the 5 became law.
  • Of 27 first-committee deaths, 15 happened in a Natural Resources committee. Both data-center tax-break restraints died in Assembly Revenue.
  • New since 2023: the governor’s veto stopped three bills in this set. Passing both houses is no longer the last step to watch.

Passed recently (2025)

SB36 · Became law

A state account to buy water rights (legal permits to use set amounts of water) in over-pumped areas and retire them for good. This idea failed in 2021 and 2023 before passing.

SB6 · Became law

Cloud seeding funded again, unanimously. Also new: SB442 (shut-off protections), SB276 (pollution-permit notice), AB449/SB326 (small-utility rates), AB104 (grant rules).

Questions for your group

1

No desalination or pipeline bill has ever been filed. Unworkable ideas — or did nobody ask a sponsor? What would a first-step study cost?

2

Cloud seeding got money twice with zero “no” votes. What made that new-supply idea easy when nothing else was tried?

3

For data centers, three levers exist: the tax deal, state water law, and local permits. Each stalled in a different place. Which lever fits what your group wants?

4

The tax-break cap passed the Senate, then died in Assembly Revenue at a deadline. Was that a “no” to the idea, or a calendar death?

5

Groundwater board reform passed unanimously and still isn’t law after two tries. What would have to change on a third try?

6

Three 2023 bills died by veto, not by vote. How does the governor’s desk change what “realistic” means?
