## How a bill moves

A **bill** is a written idea for a new law. It moves in steps:

start → first committee → floor vote → the other house → the governor.

A **committee** (a small work group of lawmakers) studies the bill first. If the committee says yes, the whole house takes a **floor vote** — every member votes yes or no. Then the bill repeats those steps in the other house. Nevada has two houses: the Assembly and the Senate. Last, the governor signs it into law. A bill can stop at any step.

## What history shows: three baskets

### Often moved before

Ideas like these often became law in this record. Most passed their final votes with few or no "no" votes.

- Opened river channel repair grants to tribes — 2023 AB19 · became law
- Made counties and cities write long-term water plans — 2019 SB150 · became law
- Banned decorative grass that uses Colorado River water in southern Nevada — 2021 AB356 · became law
- Set up a state account to buy **water rights** (legal permits to use set amounts of water) and retire them for good — 2025 SB36 · became law

### Got support but didn't finish

These passed big votes but never became law. The record does not say why. Often, time ran out.

- Changed how **groundwater** (water stored underground) boards are formed and ended — 2023 SB180 · passed both houses with zero "no" votes, still did not become law
- Planned a review of water saving, plus a fake-grass study — 2025 SB143 · passed the Senate 21-0, died in the Assembly
- Would have made the **State Engineer** (the state's top water official) use the best available science — 2023 AB387 · passed the Assembly 26-14, died in the Senate
- Would have required watch-and-fix plans before some water permits — 2019 AB30 · passed the Assembly 31-9, died in the Senate

### Rarely moved before

Ideas like these usually stopped at the first committee, more than once.

- Changed which court checks the State Engineer's decisions — 2021 SJR1 · died in first committee
- Set up **water banking** (saving unused water to use or trade later) — 2021 AB354 · died in first committee
- Created a new state advisory board for drought planning — 2019 SB499 · died in first committee
- Restructured the Colorado River Commission — 2019 SB76 · died in first committee (2021 AB15 tried again and stopped there too)

## People and process signals

Facts only. No judgment about anyone is implied.

- Committees themselves put forward about half the bills in this set (45 of 88). A **sponsor** is the lawmaker or committee that puts a bill forward.
- Pete Goicoechea (R) was the most frequent person sponsor: 13 bills from 2019 to 2023. Some became law; some stalled in committee.
- Howard Watts (D) sponsored water-saving and clean-water bills in all four sessions.
- Bills with sponsors from both major parties were rare: 4 out of 43 person-sponsored bills. Three of those four became law.
- Failed bills stopped in their first committee most often — 26 of 40 — split about evenly between the two houses.

## Passed recently (2025)

The newest session already changed some things. These are facts, not endorsements.

- **2025 SB36 · became law** — a state account to buy water rights in over-used groundwater areas and retire them for good.
- **2025 SB276 · became law** — new public notice rules for water pollution permits.

Also new in 2025: AB104 (updated grant rules for resource projects, water included) and AB132 (bigger wildlife water catchers allowed without a permit).

## Questions for your group

1. SB180 (2023) passed both houses with zero "no" votes and still did not become law. Was that a "no" to the idea, or a timing problem?
2. Retiring water rights failed in 2023 (SB176) and became law in 2025 (SB36). What changed in between?
3. Most failed bills never got past their first committee. What might help an idea get a hearing and a vote?
4. Every bill about who checks the State Engineer failed — six bills since 2021. Two recent ones (2023, 2025) won floor votes in one house before dying in the other. Is that growing support, or chance?
5. Money bills almost never lost a floor vote, yet two money asks (2023 SB102, 2025 SB159) died in committee. What does "support" mean when the committee is the filter?
6. This record never says why a bill stopped. Who was in the room and could say more?
