# Nevada water policy decision memo (2019–2025)
**Prepared for:** The Forum  
**Source:** Nevada water-scarcity Pass 2 dataset (keyword-discovered; not a proven universe of all water bills)  
**Sessions:** 80th–83rd (2019–2025)  
**Core analytic set:** 70 bills with `passes_water_title_filter=true` (stricter title match). The other 18 are noted separately as weaker/tangential hits.

---

## 1) Executive brief

### Facts
- In the full 88-bill set: **45 enacted**, **40 failed**, **3 labeled “In Progress.”** Those 3 are joint resolutions that were **enrolled and filed with the Secretary of State** (functionally adopted as resolutions, not statutes).
- In the **70 stronger title matches**: **33 enacted**, **34 failed**, **3 adopted resolutions**.
- Most failures die early: among the 34 failed strong-match bills, **21 never left origin committee**, **7 cleared committee but not floor**, and **6 cleared both chambers** but still did not become law (deadline / “no further action” endings).
- **What tends to succeed:** narrow State Engineer / groundwater *administration* fixes; water-conservation plan updates that are operational rather than rights-redefining; pollution-control permitting tweaks; and especially **appropriations / grants / dam-channel funding** (near-perfect enactment when they are straightforward money bills).
- **What tends to stall:** big structural groundwater reforms (water banks, conjunctive-management mandates, basin redesign), Colorado River Commission redesign, drought advisory architecture, and some quality measures that pick a fight (Legionnaires building standards; Tahoe bottled-water ban).

### Analysis — recommendation frame

| Frame | Issue areas |
| --- | --- |
| **Pursue** | Targeted conservation-plan / efficiency implementation (not a brand-new rights scheme); bipartisan groundwater *management-plan / process* fixes; water-quality permitting and pollution-control updates with cross-party authors; channel/dam/grant packages tied to existing accounts |
| **Adapt** | Groundwater-board governance; vested-rights adjudication; recycling/reuse studies; plastic / product bans; anything that passed one chamber with votes but died on the clock |
| **Avoid-for-now (or only with a new angle)** | Reopening just-passed pollution-permit or guzzler/appropriation process bills from 2025; CRC membership redesign; constitutional water-court shifts; broad “create new water-banking / drought board” vehicles without a narrowed coalition |

**Bottom line:** Since 2019, Nevada has repeatedly enacted **incremental water administration and conservation-implementation** bills, and has repeatedly killed or clocked out **structural reallocations of authority**. The best next ask is something that looks like a **fix of a prior success** (clear implementer, limited scope, ideally bipartisan or committee-carried), not a brand-new governance body or rights market.

### Theme-by-theme notes

#### Conservation / efficiency — **promising, with a 2025 miss**
- **Illustrative successes:** **80:AB163** (water-loss audits + WaterSense fixtures), **81:AB356**, **82:AB191**, **82:AB220**.
- **Miss:** **83:AB134** died in origin committee (conservation plans tied to perfected rights).
- **Facts:** 4/5 strong-filter conservation bills enacted; floor support high when heard.
- **Analysis:** Conservation *implementation* has a track record. **80:AB163** passed on a **party-line Assembly Final Passage (28–12, D yea / R nay)**—so conservation can win without Republican floor love if the coalition and session math work—but later conservation updates (**82:AB191**) passed **unanimously**. Prefer bills that look like **plan/audit/incentive maintenance**, not a new obligation layered onto water rights (the **AB134** shape).

#### Groundwater / water rights / State Engineer — **largest lane; split personality**
- **Success examples:** **80:SB140**, **80:SB250**, **81:AB6**, **82:SB113** (groundwater management plans; Goicoechea + GOP Senate bloc; Assembly 42–0), **83:SB36**.
- **Committee deaths / structural fails:** **80:AB51** (conjunctive management regs), **81:AB354** (water banks), **81:SJR1** (Court of Appeals water jurisdiction), multiple 2025 admin bills (**AB109**, **AB190**, **AB265**, **SB342**).
- **High-support unfinished:** **80:AB30**, **82:AB387**, **83:SB31**.
- **Analysis:** “Groundwater” is not one politics. **Narrow process and management-plan bills** move; **market/authority redesigns** and many mid-tier admin bills die in Natural Resources. Republican primary energy is concentrated here (Goicoechea et al.)—useful if The Forum’s ask can be framed as **orderly basin management**, risky if framed as **new statewide control**.

#### Local water boards / governance — **popular unfinished business**
- **82:SB180** (groundwater boards; primary Sen. Goicoechea) passed **both floors unanimously** and still failed (“No further action taken”).
- Other board bills often die in/near committee (**81:SB149**, **83:AB363**, **83:AB485**).
- **Analysis:** This is one of the clearest **adapt** opportunities: the policy is not floor-toxic; it needs **session timing, second-house shepherding, and maybe a narrower board-powers brief**.

#### Water quality / pollution control — **viable, but product/building mandates are fragile**
- **Wins:** **81:AB146**, **82:SB258** (note: temporary water-change mechanics; adjacent to rights admin), **83:SB276** (broad bipartisan primary team).
- **High-support losses:** **82:AB263** (Legionnaires; party-line Assembly yes), **83:SB324** (Tahoe plastic bottle ban, Scheible/Nguyen; Senate 16–4 then deadline).
- **Analysis:** Permitting/pollution-control updates can assemble coalitions. **Facility standards and consumer product bans** draw clearer partisan or regional resistance and are easy to run out of time.

#### Infrastructure / grants / appropriations — **highest win rate**
- Marlette Lake, South Fork Dam, DWR tools/studies, resource grants: almost always enact when structured as money/account bills.
- **Exception:** **82:SB102** (DWR appropriation) failed in committee—reminder that not every dollar bill is automatic.
- **Analysis:** Great for **coalition hygiene and proof of delivery**, weak as a standalone scarcity strategy unless tied to a policy theory (what the dollars unlock).

#### Colorado River / CRC — **resolution yes, institutional redesign no**
- **82:SJR3** adopted unanimously; **80:SB76** / **81:AB15** (CRC services/membership) died in committee; compact jurisdiction tweak **80:SB433** enacted.
- **Analysis:** Symbolic/federal-ask resolutions are easy. **Touching CRC membership or service authority is not.**

#### Drought planning architecture & recycling studies — **thin record, early deaths**
- **80:SB499** (drought resiliency advisory board) — committee death.
- **80:AB265** (DRI treatment/recycling study) — cleared committee energy but no floor enactment path completed.
- **Analysis:** Needs redesign: smaller study ask, named funder, or hitch onto an appropriations vehicle.

## 4) Vote & legislator signals

### Floor Final Passage — bipartisan vs party-line

**Facts**
- Appropriations / grant bills: consistently **bipartisan unanimous or near-unanimous**.
- Groundwater management-plan style bills (e.g. **82:SB113**): **bipartisan unanimous** on the recorded best Final Passage.
- Conservation is mixed: **80:AB163** Assembly Final Passage was **party-line (D 28–0 yea, R 0–12 nay)** yet the bill enacted; **82:AB191** was **42–0**.
- Quality/building/product bills more often show **Democratic yea / Republican nay** patterns when contested (**82:AB263**, **81:AJR3**).

**Analysis:** Floor “popularity” is common for water bills that escape committee. Party-line opposition appears when the bill looks like **mandate/liability expansion** (building safety water systems, broad conservation obligations) rather than **agency process**. Unanimous bipartisan floors did **not** save **82:SB180**—so bipartisan votes are necessary but not sufficient without deadline management.

### Recurring champions (strong-filter primary sponsors, people)

| Legislator | Party | Approx. primary count (strong-filter) | Signal |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Sen. Pete Goicoechea | R | 12 | Dominant groundwater/rural water carrier; also appears on some bipartisan deals |
| Asm. Howard Watts | D | 4 | Conservation and quality; cross-aisle with Republicans on **83:SB276** |
| Sen. Chris Brooks | D | 3 | Early conservation partner on **80:AB163**; with Goicoechea on **80:SB236** |
| Sens. Settelmeyer / Ohrenschall; Asm. Gurr | R/D/R | 3 each | Recurring but less theme-defining in this set |

Committee vehicles matter as much as people: **Assembly Natural Resources** (and earlier NR/Ag/Mining), **Senate Natural Resources**, plus **Finance / Ways & Means** for money bills.

**Cross-party sponsorship is rare but real** on a few enacted bills (**80:AB233**, **80:SB236**, **83:SB276**) and even on the failed recycling study (**80:AB265**). Durable pattern: **Goicoechea + Democratic urban partners** on selected process bills.

### Committee signals (cautious)

**Facts**
- Many hearings are logged as **“Heard, No Action”** or **“Heard”** without a reported do-pass—this is the dominant soft-kill pattern.
- Advance language clusters around **“Do pass”** / **“Amend, and do pass as amended.”**
- Roll-call detail exists for only a minority of committee actions; some Yea rows in the dataset are **inferred from membership minus Nay/Absent**. Do not treat committee scorecards as complete.

**Analysis:** Natural Resources committees are the **choke point** for scarcity policy. Finance committees are the **on-ramp** for funding. A Forum strategy that never pre-wires Natural Resources hearing priority is fighting the historical failure mode.

### High-support failures that look like timing/process—not policy rejection

1. **82:SB180** — unanimous Senate *and* Assembly Final Passage; still died.  
2. **83:SB31**, **83:SB324**, **83:SB143** — Joint Rule 14.3.x end-of-session stops after clear Senate majorities.  
3. **80:AB30**, **82:AB387** — Assembly majority then rule-deadline death.

These are the strongest “**refile with earlier second-house movement**” candidates.

## 5) Strategy implications for the next ask

### Good bets
1. **Conservation implementation 2.0** — audit/plan/incentive follow-through in the spirit of **80:AB163 / 82:AB191**, avoiding perfected-rights conditionality that sank **83:AB134**.  
2. **Groundwater management-plan / basin-process cleanup** modeled on **82:SB113** and **83:SB36**, ideally with Goicoechea-lane credibility *or* a true bipartisan pair.  
3. **Pollution-control permitting maintenance** adjacent to **83:SB276**, only if there is a clear remaining gap (do not reopen the same fight).  
4. **Board-governance refile** built from **82:SB180**’s unanimous votes, introduced early, with a dedicated Assembly *and* Senate floor manager.  
5. **Funding wrapper** (channel clearance, dam safety, DWR tools) as coalition glue—not the whole agenda.

### Crowded / recently enacted
- **83:SB276** discharge permitting  
- **83:AB132** guzzler exemption  
- **83:SB36** appropriation/change process  
- **83:AB104** DCNR grant regs  
- Fresh dam/channel account tweaks (**AB26**, **SB108**)

### Needs redesign
| Failed shape | Why it looks hard | Redesign idea |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Water banks / conjunctive mandate | Dies in committee; large structural change | Pilot basin; voluntary tools; study-to-draft sequence |
| CRC membership/services overhaul | Repeated committee death | Stick to federal-ask resolutions unless CRC asks for the bill |
| New drought advisory board | No traction | Give duties to existing interim committee (note **SB143** also clocked out—needs earlier move) |
| Recycling study | Bipartisan interest, weak completion | Turn into a funded line item in a DWR/DRI appropriation |
| Product/building mandates | Party splits + deadline risk | Phase-ins, narrower geography, industry negotiation before intro |

### Watch list
- **Groundwater boards** near-miss (**82:SB180**)  
- **Vested-rights adjudication** (**83:SB31**)  
- **Tahoe bottle ban** (**83:SB324**) — majority but fragile  
- Adopted resolutions (**81:AJR2**, **81:AJR3**, **82:SJR3**) as messaging cover, not statutory progress  
- Weaker-filter land-use bills that nonetheless pass (session packaging opportunities)

## Method caveats (read before over-fitting)

1. Keyword discovery ≠ complete water universe; conclusions are about **this retrieved set**.  
2. Strong-filter (70) should drive strategy; weaker 18 inflate “enacted” counts with tangential measures.  
3. Themes are human-assigned from digests for decision support; a few digests are thin (especially pure appropriation one-liners).  
4. Committee vote rolls are incomplete; some Yeas are inferred—used only directionally.  
5. “In Progress” resolutions in the database are **adopted filings**, not live bill fights.  
6. Primary vs co-sponsor labels in the appendix use the corrected NELIS scrape from PR `#17` (`bill-sponsors.json` with 131 primary / 51 cosponsor rows). Merge that PR before treating sponsors on `main` as authoritative.
